  <h2 class="text-3xl font-black tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-12 mb-6">The Business Foundation: Decisions to Make Before Writing Code</h2>

  <p>
    Building the technical system before settling the business model is the most common mistake. You end up with infrastructure that doesn't match your incentive structure, or worse, a referral program nobody uses because the rewards aren't compelling enough to motivate sharing.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Decision 1: One-Sided vs. Double-Sided Incentives</h3>

  <p>
    <strong>One-sided</strong> — only the referrer gets rewarded. Common in B2B where the referrer is a trusted advisor and their recommendation carries enough weight on its own.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Double-sided</strong> — both the referrer and the new customer get rewarded. More friction to set up, but consistently outperforms one-sided programs in B2C and early-stage B2B SaaS. The new customer has a tangible reason to act on the recommendation instead of filing it away as "something to look at later."
  </p>

  <El as="alert" type="info">
    Dropbox's famous referral program was double-sided — both parties got free storage. That single design decision drove 3,900% growth in 15 months. The lesson isn't "copy Dropbox." It's that removing the friction for the referred user is as important as motivating the referrer.
  </El>

  <p>
    <strong>Rule of thumb:</strong> If your product is self-serve and the buying decision takes less than a week, use double-sided. If you have a longer sales cycle or the referrer's credibility is the primary driver, one-sided works.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Decision 2: Cash Commission vs. Credits vs. Discount</h3>

  <div class="w-full overflow-x-auto rounded-2xl border border-slate-200 shadow-sm mb-8">
    <table class="min-w-[600px] w-full divide-y divide-slate-200 m-0 text-left">
      <thead class="bg-slate-50">
      <tr>
        <th class="px-6 py-4 text-xs font-bold uppercase text-slate-500">Reward Type</th>
        <th class="px-6 py-4 text-xs font-bold uppercase text-slate-500">Best For</th>
        <th class="px-6 py-4 text-xs font-bold uppercase text-slate-500">Risk</th>
      </tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody class="divide-y divide-slate-100 bg-white text-sm text-slate-600">
      <tr>
        <td class="px-6 py-4 font-bold text-slate-900">Cash commission (recurring)</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">B2B SaaS, higher ACV products</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Higher cost, attracts professional affiliates who need management</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td class="px-6 py-4 font-bold text-slate-900">Account credits</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Usage-based, freemium products</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Low perceived value if credits don't translate to real savings</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td class="px-6 py-4 font-bold text-slate-900">Discount for referred user</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Self-serve, price-sensitive markets</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Trains customers to expect discounts, can cheapen brand perception</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td class="px-6 py-4 font-bold text-slate-900">Flat fee per conversion</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">Simple programs, one-time purchases</td>
        <td class="px-6 py-4">No ongoing motivation for referrers once they've earned the flat fee</td>
      </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </div>

  <p>
    For most SaaS products the highest-converting structure is: <strong>referrer earns 20–30% recurring commission + referred user gets 10–20% off their first invoice.</strong> Both parties win immediately and the referrer stays motivated to send quality leads because their income depends on referred users staying.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Decision 3: Who Can Refer?</h3>

  <ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">
    <li><strong>All users:</strong> Maximum reach, minimum friction. Every signup gets a referral link automatically. Works well for self-serve products with low fraud risk.</li>
    <li><strong>Opted-in users only:</strong> Users apply or opt in to become referrers. Higher quality referrals, lower fraud risk, but significantly lower referral volume. Better for high-ACV products where one bad referral creates a support burden.</li>
    <li><strong>Curated affiliates:</strong> You invite specific users, influencers, or partners. Highest quality, lowest volume, most management overhead. Appropriate for enterprise products or specific partnership programs.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    For early-stage SaaS: <strong>all users, opt-in at dashboard.</strong> Every user gets a referral link they can activate with one click. You're not curating at this stage — you're maximizing reach with minimal overhead.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Decision 4: Attribution Window</h3>

  <p>How long after clicking a referral link should a conversion still be credited to the referrer?</p>

  <ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">
    <li><strong>7 days:</strong> Too short for SaaS. Most users evaluate products for longer than a week before paying.</li>
    <li><strong>30 days:</strong> The standard. Covers most evaluation cycles.</li>
    <li><strong>90 days:</strong> Better for high-ACV products with longer sales cycles.</li>
    <li><strong>Lifetime:</strong> The referred customer is permanently attributed to the referrer. Every future payment generates a commission. High referrer motivation, complex to manage at scale.</li>
  </ul>

  <El as="alert" type="info">
    Default recommendation: 30-day cookie window for attribution, with lifetime recurring commissions on subscription renewals regardless of whether the cookie is still active. The attribution window is about the initial conversion — once the customer is in your system, the commission relationship should persist.
  </El>

</section>

export function useReferralLink() {
  const { user } = useUser();

    if (!user?.referralSlug) return null;
    const baseUrl = process.env.NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_URL ?? window.location.origin;
    return \`\${baseUrl}?ref=\${user.referralSlug}\`;
  }, [user?.referralSlug]);

      return { referralLink };
    }`}

  <CodeBlock

    lang="typescript"
    code={`// lib/referral/generate-slug.ts
import { customAlphabet } from 'nanoid';

// Alphanumeric only, no ambiguous characters (0, O, I, l)
const generateId = customAlphabet('23456789ABCDEFGHJKLMNPQRSTUVWXYZabcdefghjkmnpqrstuvwxyz', 8);

    export function generateReferralSlug(username?: string): string {
      if (username) {
        // Use username if available and valid, fall back to random ID
        const cleaned = username.toLowerCase().replace(/[^a-z0-9-]/g, '').slice(0, 20);
        if (cleaned.length >= 3) return cleaned;
      }
      return generateId();
    }`}

<section id="attribution" class="scroll-mt-28 space-y-4">

  <h2 class="text-3xl font-black tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-12 mb-6">Layer 2: Attribution — Capturing the Referral in React</h2>

  <p>
    When a visitor arrives via a referral link, capture the <code>?ref=</code> parameter immediately and persist it in a cookie. This needs to happen before anything else — before the user reads the landing page, before they navigate to a different section, before the session has any chance to lose the parameter.
  </p>

  <CodeBlock
    filename="components/ReferralTracker.tsx"
    lang="tsx"
    code={`// components/ReferralTracker.tsx
'use client';

    import { useEffect } from 'react';
    import { useSearchParams } from 'next/navigation';

    const COOKIE_NAME = 'affiliate_ref';
    const COOKIE_MAX_AGE = 60 * 60 * 24 * 30; // 30 days

    function isValidSlug(slug: string): boolean {
      return /^[a-zA-Z0-9][a-zA-Z0-9-]{1,39}$/.test(slug);
    }

    function getCookie(name: string): string | null {
      const match = document.cookie.match(new RegExp(\`(^| )\${name}=([^;]+)\`));
      return match ? decodeURIComponent(match[2]) : null;
    }

    function setCookie(name: string, value: string, maxAge: number): void {
      document.cookie = [
        \`\${name}=\${encodeURIComponent(value)}\`,
        \`max-age=\${maxAge}\`,
        'path=/',
        'SameSite=Lax',
        process.env.NODE_ENV === 'production' ? 'Secure' : '',
      ]
        .filter(Boolean)
        .join('; ');
    }

    export function ReferralTracker() {
      const searchParams = useSearchParams();

      useEffect(() => {
        const ref = searchParams.get('ref');
        if (!ref || !isValidSlug(ref)) return;

        // First-click attribution — don't overwrite if already set
        if (getCookie(COOKIE_NAME)) return;

        setCookie(COOKIE_NAME, ref, COOKIE_MAX_AGE);
      }, [searchParams]);

      return null;
    }`}
  />

  <p>Mount this in your root layout, wrapped in <code>Suspense</code>:</p>

  <CodeBlock
    filename="app/layout.tsx"
    lang="tsx"
    code={`// app/layout.tsx
import { Suspense } from 'react';
import { ReferralTracker } from '@/components/ReferralTracker';

export default function RootLayout({ children }: { children: React.ReactNode }) {
  return (
    <html lang="en">
      <body>
        <Suspense fallback={null}>
          <ReferralTracker />
        </Suspense>
        {children}
      </body>
    </html>
  );
}`}
  />

    <code>useSearchParams()</code> in the App Router requires the component to be wrapped in <code>Suspense</code> or it will throw during static rendering. The <code>fallback={null}</code> means no UI change — the tracker is invisible to the user.

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Handling the OAuth Signup Gap</h3>

  <p>
    The most common attribution failure in React referral systems is the OAuth flow. When a user clicks a referral link then signs up via Google OAuth:
  </p>

  <ol class="list-decimal pl-6 space-y-2">
    <li>They land on your page — cookie set ✅</li>
    <li>They click "Sign up with Google" — redirect to Google</li>
    <li>Google redirects back to your callback URL — the <code>?ref=</code> param is gone</li>
    <li>Cookie is still there ✅ — attribution survives</li>

    The cookie persists through OAuth redirects as long as the callback URL is on your domain. The only failure case is if your OAuth callback clears cookies or redirects to a subdomain — check this specifically in your auth implementation.
  </p>

  <h2 class="text-3xl font-black tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-12 mb-6">Layer 3: Conversion — Stripe Integration</h2>

  <p>
    When a user initiates checkout, read the referral cookie server-side and attach it to the Stripe session as metadata. The conversion is then confirmed via webhook, not the client-side success page.
  </p>

  <CodeBlock
    filename="app/api/checkout/route.ts"
    lang="typescript"
    code={`// app/api/checkout/route.ts
import { NextRequest, NextResponse } from 'next/server';
import Stripe from 'stripe';

const stripe = new Stripe(process.env.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY!, {
  apiVersion: '2024-11-20',
});

    export async function POST(request: NextRequest): Promise<NextResponse> {
      const affiliateRef = request.cookies.get('affiliate_ref')?.value ?? null;

      const session = await stripe.checkout.sessions.create({
        mode: 'subscription',
        line_items: [{ price: process.env.STRIPE_PRICE_ID!, quantity: 1 }],
        success_url: \`\${process.env.NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_URL}/dashboard?session_id={CHECKOUT_SESSION_ID}\`,
        cancel_url: \`\${process.env.NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_URL}/pricing\`,
        metadata: {
          affiliate_ref: affiliateRef ?? '',
        },
        // Attach to subscription for recurring commission tracking
        ...(affiliateRef && {
          subscription_data: {
            metadata: { affiliate_ref: affiliateRef },
          },
        }),
      });

      return NextResponse.json({ url: session.url });
    }`}
  />

  <CodeBlock
    filename="app/api/webhooks/stripe/route.ts"
    lang="typescript"
    code={`// app/api/webhooks/stripe/route.ts
import { NextRequest, NextResponse } from 'next/server';
import Stripe from 'stripe';
import { db } from '@/lib/db';

const stripe = new Stripe(process.env.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY!, {
  apiVersion: '2024-11-20',
});

    export async function POST(request: NextRequest): Promise<NextResponse> {
      const body = await request.text();
      const sig = request.headers.get('stripe-signature');

      if (!sig) return NextResponse.json({ error: 'No signature' }, { status: 400 });

      let event: Stripe.Event;
      try {
        event = stripe.webhooks.constructEvent(body, sig, process.env.STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET!);
      } catch {
        return NextResponse.json({ error: 'Invalid signature' }, { status: 400 });
      }

      if (event.type === 'checkout.session.completed') {
        const session = event.data.object as Stripe.Checkout.Session;
        const ref = session.metadata?.affiliate_ref;

        if (ref && session.payment_status === 'paid') {
          // Idempotency guard
          const exists = await db.referralConversion.findUnique({
            where: { stripeSessionId: session.id },
          });
          if (!exists) {
            await db.referralConversion.create({
              data: {
                referralSlug: ref,
                stripeSessionId: session.id,
                stripeCustomerId: session.customer as string,
                amountCents: session.amount_total ?? 0,
                commissionCents: Math.floor((session.amount_total ?? 0) * 0.2),
                status: 'pending',
                convertedAt: new Date(),
              },
            });
          }
        }
      }

      // Recurring commissions on subscription renewals
      if (event.type === 'invoice.payment_succeeded') {
        const invoice = event.data.object as Stripe.Invoice;
        if (invoice.billing_reason !== 'subscription_cycle') {
          return NextResponse.json({ received: true });
        }

        const sub = await stripe.subscriptions.retrieve(invoice.subscription as string);
        const ref = sub.metadata?.affiliate_ref;

        if (ref && invoice.amount_paid) {
          await db.referralCommission.create({
            data: {
              referralSlug: ref,
              stripeInvoiceId: invoice.id,
              amountCents: invoice.amount_paid,
              commissionCents: Math.floor(invoice.amount_paid * 0.2),
              status: 'pending',
              paidAt: new Date(),
            },
          });
        }
      }

      return NextResponse.json({ received: true });
    }`}

  <h2 class="text-3xl font-black tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-12 mb-6">The Business Metrics That Tell You If It's Working</h2>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Referral Rate</h3>
  <blockquote>
    Referral Rate = Customers acquired via referral / Total new customers
  </blockquote>
  <p>
    Healthy range: <strong>15–25%</strong> for a well-run SaaS referral program. Below 10% — the incentive is too weak or the program isn't visible enough. Above 30% early on — you're in a tight-knit community, lean into it hard.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Referrer Activation Rate</h3>
  <blockquote>
    Activation Rate = Users who shared at least once / Users with a referral link
  </blockquote>
  <p>
    If less than 20% of users with referral links ever share them, the reward isn't compelling or the link is too hard to find. Add the referral link to your onboarding flow, post-payment confirmation page, and a persistent dashboard widget.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)</h3>
  <blockquote>
    K = Invites sent per user × Conversion rate of those invites
  </blockquote>
  <ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">
    <li>K &lt; 0.1: Referral program has minimal impact on growth</li>
    <li>K = 0.3–0.5: Meaningful contribution, cuts CAC materially</li>
    <li>K &gt; 1.0: Viral growth — user base grows without external acquisition</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Most SaaS products land between 0.1–0.3. Moving from 0.1 to 0.2 cuts your effective CAC in half. That's the goal — not virality, but meaningful CAC reduction.
  </p>

  <h3 class="text-2xl font-bold tracking-tight text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-4">Referred User LTV vs. Organic User LTV</h3>
  <p>
    Referred users consistently show 15–25% higher LTV in SaaS products. They arrive with social proof from someone they trust, lower skepticism, and higher intent. If your referred users are churning faster than organic users, the referral targeting is wrong — your referrers are sending unqualified traffic.
  </p>
